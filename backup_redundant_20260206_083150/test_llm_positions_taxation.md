### Detailed Position Analysis

#### Public Funding

Total responses
: 64 positions extracted across 3 distinct responses

Support position
: 60 responses (93.8%), 45.0% express strong advocacy

Primary stakeholders (support)
: EU Citizens (17), NGOs (15), Companies (9)

Core arguments (support)
: EU should establish a dedicated funding instrument to bridge the gap between academic research and operational maintenance of open source ecosystems.; Open-source strategy advances autonomy, strong privacy protection and open innovation without compromising global interoperability and connectivity.

Opposition position
: 3 responses (4.7%), 0.0% express strong opposition

Core arguments (oppose)
: Current funding system for OSS is inefficient and leads to neglect of existing code.; The EU open-source sector lacks institutions due to neglect of policymakers, making it difficult for charitable entities like InÖG to form and serve the public interest.

Specific proposals mentioned
: 1-2 billion via Digital Europe Programme (1 mentions); Dedicated maintenance grants (1 mentions); Direct Sponsorship mechanism modeled after GitHub Sponsors (1 mentions)

Evidence cited
: https://openssf.org/blog/2025/09/23/open-infrastructure-is-not-free-a-joint-statement-on-sustainable-stewardship/ (2); Evidence from French public-sector initiatives (2); In our own experience we could not form a charitable entity that is legally binding beholden to serving the public interest in the field of civic-tech (1); Rejoingnat des structures de mutualisation telles que CSI Piemonte en Italie ou le TOSIT en France (1); impatto del software è spesso sottostimato dai ricercatori stessi (1)

#### Tax Incentive

Total responses
: 44 positions extracted across 3 distinct responses

Support position
: 31 responses (70.5%), 3.2% express strong advocacy

Primary stakeholders (support)
: EU Citizens (10), NGOs (6), ACADEMIC_RESEARCH_INSTITTUTION (5)

Core arguments (support)
: Making donations tax-deductible would break a main barrier for Free and Open Source projects.; Granting public interest status to associations or foundations that run open source projects would exempt them from VAT and enable tax deductions for donors and subscribers.

Opposition position
: 11 responses (25.0%), 0.0% express strong opposition

Core arguments (oppose)
: Mainstream EDA vendors prohibit publishing any outputs from their tools, killing open hardware at its root.; Tax breaks for OSS adoption/development should be carefully considered to prevent corporate co-opting.

Specific proposals mentioned
: Create a favorable fiscal regime to contributions, donations and other forms of support given by organizations; (1 mentions); Migration Grant covering a percentage of the annual ICT budget for administrations adopting EU-approved licences. (1 mentions); officially declare the associated work as of public interest, making donations tax-deductible (1 mentions)

Evidence cited
: Innovation is far easier when knowledge is shared freely, and improvements in open-source programs come to the benefit of all. (1); Roadmap §3.3, §7 (1); White Paper §3-4 (1); Thus establishing other forms of contributions outside of code like engagement in the project or collaboration agreements suffer from severe friction due to different legal systems and recent geopolitical developments (1); corporate lobbying under the open source banner (1)

#### Digital Services Tax

Total responses
: 19 positions extracted across 3 distinct responses

Support position
: 13 responses (68.4%), 15.4% express strong advocacy

Primary stakeholders (support)
: EU Citizens (6), Companies (3), NGOs (2)

Core arguments (support)
: The current technology is locked and controlled by a few third parties, making it essential to break software into manageable parts.; The EU should tax proprietary vendors to incentivize code opening.

Opposition position
: 5 responses (26.3%), 20.0% express strong opposition

Core arguments (oppose)
: A digital services tax on proprietary vendors could create a lock-in effect and hinder innovation.; Permissive licenses allow publicly-funded innovations to be absorbed by proprietary vendors effectively undermining the economic sustainability of the open-source ecosystem.

Specific proposals mentioned
: Mandatory SBOM publication for all public sector vendors even closed-source (1 mentions)

Evidence cited
: EESCs report on services of general interest (TEN/851-EESC-2025) (2); software supply chain (1); Examples of banks requiring Android/iOS apps (1); White Paper §3-4 (1); Roadmap §3.3, §7 (1)
