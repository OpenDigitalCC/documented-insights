Eng - Contribution to the European Consulta-
tion on Open Source

Introduction
This document aims to share experiences regarding the integration of open-source solutions in
the healthcare sector. By presenting these insights, we wish to highlight the lessons learned and
the tangible benefits of this approach, while identifying the challenges that need to be addressed
for broader adoption.

1. Operational Experience from a Healthcare Facility
Implementation of Open Source in a Healthcare Facility for 20 Years
Since January 2025, increasing tensions with the U.S. administration have prompted many
public entities in France to clarify their dependence on American digital tools.
A hospital or healthcare professional can be paralyzed not by an attack, but by a simple
administrative decision from a foreign government implemented by private companies. This
raises questions about the necessity for continuity, which is essential for providing quality
healthcare services.
In this context, the adoption of free software has taken on a strategic dimension for a hospital
center in Martinique. These tools cannot be "unplugged" by a foreign actor, thus ensuring data
control and service continuity.
The experience of this facility, which has operated autonomously with open-source solutions
for 20 years, proves that this independence is both technically feasible and economically viable.
What was once a relevant technical choice is now a strategic imperative for Europe.
Technical Maturity and Operational Resilience.
Major open-source solutions (Linux, Samba, Docker) have reached a maturity level comparable
to proprietary alternatives. In this healthcare facility, 20 years of operation without major inci-
dents demonstrate this reliability. In case of hardware failures, the system continues to function
in degraded mode, ensuring a return to normal operations without data loss.
Rapid Reconstruction Capability.
In the event of a destructive cyberattack, an infrastructure based on open-source components
can be quickly restored on standard hardware. The absence of dependency on licenses allows
for completely autonomous operation, without fear of being cut off by a foreign government.
Regular Reconstruction: Proven Resilience.
The infrastructure of the facility is systematically rebuilt every three years, including server
renewals and installation of the latest versions of tools. This process is proving less costly than
using proprietary solutions.
Massive Structural Savings.
Over the past 20 years, savings on licenses amount to several hundred thousand euros. These
savings are crucial for freeing up budgets for investment and training.
Decision-Making and Technical Autonomy.
Open source provides decision-making independence, allowing for objective evaluation of
proprietary solutions when necessary.
Synergy with Artificial Intelligence and the Open Ecosystem.
The emergence of generative AIs is changing the game for custom development, making it
possible to create tailored tools in a matter of days, thus fostering a dynamic conducive to
innovation.
Examples of Developed Tools
Public Hospital Sector
Creation of a complete user management and automation system, with an interface integrated
into the HR system, ensuring perfect alignment with operational needs.
Business Continuity
Development of a degraded procedure tool for the Electronic Patient Record, allowing facilities
to maintain medical activity even when the central EPR is unavailable.
Email Resilience
Creation of an automated deployment tool for an open-source email server, ensuring a resilient
backup solution in the event of a cyberattack.

2. Experience Sharing in ARS

Opening Up to Open Source for Healthcare Facilities
Regional Health Agencies (ARS) have several missions, including strengthening the cybersecu-
rity of health structures. They aim to improve regional collaborations to respond in a coordinated
manner to cybersecurity incidents and to establish a secure policy aimed at healthcare facilities
and professionals.
The integration of open source into the agency's processes illustrates a modern vision and a
commitment to innovate while promoting access to health technologies. This choice allows for
the development of tools tailored to professionals and guarantees data transparency, serving as
an example for other institutions.
This initiative has also improved access to health tools in our region while reducing costs
associated with proprietary software.

Transparency and Data Security
In a context where patient trust is essential, the transparency of open-source software can
enhance this trust.
Open source promotes transparency, allowing each user to examine the code. This transparency
is critical for managing health data. However, it is important to note that open-source solutions
also come with challenges, which are addressed in a later section.
In terms of security, the auditability of code can lead to rapid identification of vulnerabilities,
providing better protection for data. In contrast to proprietary solutions, which may contain
hidden flaws, open source allows for a greater responsiveness to threats.

Cost Reduction
The adoption of open-source solutions can lead to significant savings. Lower license costs
facilitate efficient resource reallocation, thus improving service quality without compromising
security.
However, it should be noted that savings can sometimes be offset by additional costs associated
with training and technical support needed to manage these solutions.
Therefore, open source should be considered a more sovereign and collaborative approach to
technology.

Digital Sovereignty
The French administration strongly supports open source through various initiatives, contribut-
ing to the improvement of the open-source ecosystem. To ensure the sustainability of these
efforts, support at the European level is crucial.

3. Challenges and Proposals
Challenges
Skill Shortages and Inadequate Training.
Teams need to acquire specific skills to customize and maintain open-source software, which
can require significant initial investment. Moreover, the diversity of open-source tools can lead
to integration issues with existing systems, hindering their deployment and incurring additional
costs. Training programs, such as BTS or engineering schools, remain primarily focused on
proprietary solutions. In overseas territories, this situation is exacerbated by geographical dis-
tance. Students often arrive "formatted" and with limited exposure to free alternatives. Recruiting
qualified individuals skilled in the open-source ecosystem, such as Proxmox virtualization or
Samba AD administration, presents a considerable challenge.
Centralization of Skills.
In smaller organizations, open-source expertise often relies on a single individual. This creates
a significant organizational risk and complicates efforts to raise awareness among management
about the need to expand teams. The absence of apparent crises (such as incidents) makes this
investment less of a priority for decision-makers.
Lack of Framework for Contribution.
It is crucial to consider community support. Unlike proprietary solutions, which often benefit
from dedicated customer service, support for open-source software primarily comes from the
community, which can result in longer resolution times. While internally developed tools can
be very useful, the lack of internal organization to maintain these open-source projects limits
their sharing with other hospitals, often resulting in their deposition in private repositories and
depriving the community of concrete feedback.
Pressure for Uniformity.
Mutualization logic, present in many sectors, often promotes the adoption of proprietary solu-
tions, which are perceived as less costly to maintain. While these choices may reduce some
costs, they also lead to a certain uniformity of practices and favor proprietary tools, neglecting
the specificities of certain establishments. This disadvantages open-source pioneers.
Purchasing Centers: An Inadequate Model.
Purchasing centers proposed by states aim to regulate the purchases of public entities, but they
also penalize users of open-source software. They often do not offer specific equipment, which
is generally more suitable and less expensive than that from major brands, required to maintain
an open infrastructure. Consequently, the proven model of our facility, which has worked
effectively for 20 years, faces threats from administrative constraints rather than technical ones.
Risking the abandonment of functional and cost-effective solutions under the guise of "purchase
rationalization" is concerning.
Inadequate Certifications.
Certification processes highlighted by many IT solution providers often favor proprietary pub-
lishers, involving complex requirements to obtain certifications. This stifles the development
of smaller players who could compete in terms of performance with solutions deemed more
mature.
Security Issues.
Managing the supply chain of open-source software can be complex, requiring rigorous moni-
toring of dependencies and security updates. While proprietary solutions provide a centralized
view of vulnerabilities, often at a high cost of transparency, open source ensures better re-
sponsiveness to threats, though its management becomes increasingly intricate in a regulated
environment like healthcare.

Proposals
1. Make Open Source a Strategic Sovereignty Tool.
Create or Fund the Development of European Alternatives.
Public services should have national alternatives for crucial services like messaging, cloud, or
payment systems. It is essential to support the development of free alternatives through public
procurement and to fund existing "fork" projects in Europe to share resources effectively.
Impose Interoperability and Data Portability.
Solutions adopted in the public sector should guarantee complete data portability, ensuring
autonomy in case of a rupture with a supplier for technical or geopolitical reasons.
2. Improve Training and Awareness of Open Source.
Create Specific Curricula for Free Technologies.
Recognized training programs at the European level could develop certifications targeting skills
in the use of open-source solutions.
Launch Institutional Awareness Campaigns.
These campaigns would aim to raise awareness among public officials about the stakes of digital
sovereignty and highlight the advantages of open source during instances of access deprivation
to digital tools.
3. Better Reward Contributions.
Create Compensation Mechanisms.
It is vital to allow long-term engagement with commonly used open-source projects. The primary
obstacle to contribution is not lack of desire, but rather the time allocated to these projects.
Grants or long-term funding could convert users into active contributors.
4. Reform Public Markets and Purchasing Centers.
Promote Availability of Tools Compatible with Open Source Software.
Public purchasing centers mainly offer equipment from major American manufacturers. The
EU should expand choices in purchasing centers to include hardware better suited for open
infrastructures.
Integrate Sovereignty Criteria into Public Markets.
It is important to emphasize criteria such as compatibility with open-source solutions, absence
of technological lock-in, operational autonomy, and hosting within European territory. Markets
that exclude open-source solutions should be deemed non-compliant.
Protect Proven Solutions from Forced Uniformity.
The EU must encourage the recognition of resilient infrastructures within member states.
Mutualization should not lead to the abandonment of functional solutions that guarantee data
sovereignty and security over the long term.

   Develop and maintain free alternatives, as this activity is crucial for many organizations,
   sometimes representing an obstacle to their maturation.

4. Conclusion
The experience gained over 20 years of managing a fully open-source hospital information
system clearly demonstrates that this approach is not only viable but often superior in terms
of resilience, costs, and autonomy. The main identified obstacles are primarily organizational
and cultural concerns.
Open source is now a relevant option that can become a pillar of European sovereignty. To
maintain technical control over our infrastructures, it is crucial to invest heavily in training,
subsidize strategic open-source projects, and adapt regulations to promote the adoption of
free solutions across all public services. This control can only be achieved with tools whose
functionality is understood, modifiable, and free from external control.
It is also essential to promote the use of AI, particularly to accelerate the development of tailored
open-source solutions while supporting the creation of sovereign or open-source AI to avoid
recreating strategic dependencies.
